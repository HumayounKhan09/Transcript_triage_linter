Agent: Let me walk through it practically. If a homeowners insurance company becomes insolvent, the state's insurance guaranty association typically steps in. Every state has one. In Florida, it's the Florida Insurance Guaranty Association, also known as FIGA. They take over claims handling and policy obligations up to certain limits.
Caller: So my coverage might continue even if the company goes bankrupt?
Agent: In many cases, yes — FIGA would cover claims and maintain some level of coverage during the transition. However, your policy would not simply continue indefinitely. FIGA manages the run-off period, but you would need to find new coverage relatively quickly, typically within sixty to ninety days.
Caller: And what does Meridian Home Lending do in that situation?
Agent: We monitor insurance coverage on all loans we service. If we detect that your insurance has lapsed, we'll attempt to notify you. We give you a window — typically thirty to forty-five days — to provide proof of new insurance coverage.
Caller: And if I can't get new coverage in time?
Agent: If you can't provide proof of coverage within that window, we are required by your loan agreement to purchase what's called lender-placed insurance, or force-placed insurance. This protects the lender's interest in the property.
Caller: I've heard that's really expensive.
Agent: It is considerably more expensive than regular homeowners insurance, yes. Lender-placed insurance premiums can be two to five times higher than a standard policy. It also only covers the lender's interest — your personal property and liability are not covered. It's truly a last resort.

Agent: It would be charged to your loan account. If you have an escrow account, the premium would be disbursed from there, potentially causing a shortfall that increases your monthly payment. It's in everyone's interest — yours and ours — to avoid this.
