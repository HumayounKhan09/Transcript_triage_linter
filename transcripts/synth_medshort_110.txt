Agent: Exactly. Those fees get spread over the assumed life of the loan — thirty years in your case — and layered on top of the note rate mathematically. The result is a higher effective rate that theoretically captures the total cost of borrowing.
Caller: Why theoretically?
Agent: Because the APR calculation makes assumptions that may not match your actual situation. It assumes you keep the loan for the full thirty-year term. If you sell the house or refinance in seven years, the way the fees are amortized changes, so the real cost of that loan would be different. APR is a useful comparison tool but not a perfect predictor of actual cost.
Caller: So if I had paid more fees, the APR would be even higher?
Agent: Correct. Two loans with the same note rate but different fee structures will have different APRs. A loan with no fees would have an APR equal to the note rate. That's why the regulation requires lenders to disclose APR — so you can see the true cost including fees, not just the headline rate.
Caller: Why wouldn't the lender just tell you the total fees?
Agent: They do — your closing disclosure itemizes all your fees. But APR was designed as a quick single-number comparison so that a consumer shopping between lenders can compare on an equal basis. The challenge is that different lenders include slightly different fees in their APR calculation, which can make even that comparison imperfect.

Agent: There's a standardized definition of which fees must be included, but there are gray areas.
