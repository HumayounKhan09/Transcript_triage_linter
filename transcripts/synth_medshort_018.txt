Agent: Adding someone to the mortgage note itself — the actual loan — is a different matter. The mortgage is a legal contract between you and the lender. To add a new borrower to the loan, the loan would need to be refinanced with both of you as co-borrowers.
Caller: So I can't just call you and say, add my wife?
Agent: Unfortunately not. The lender cannot simply add a borrower to an existing loan without underwriting that person, because adding a borrower changes the creditworthiness profile of the loan.
Caller: What does refinancing involve?
Agent: Refinancing means originating a new loan together. Both you and your wife would apply as co-borrowers. The lender would review both of your credit scores, income, debts, and assets. The new loan would pay off your existing one. You'd also pay closing costs, which typically run between two and five percent of the loan amount.
Caller: What's my current rate? Would refinancing make sense financially?
Agent: Your current rate is 3.875%, which is a rate that many borrowers locked in before rates rose significantly. Current market rates are considerably higher. Refinancing to add a co-borrower would mean taking on the current market rate.
Caller: So I'd lose my 3.875% rate?
Agent: Correct. A refinance would replace your existing loan with a new one at current rates.
Caller: That doesn't sound appealing. Is there any way to add her to the loan without refinancing?
Agent: There is one rare scenario called a loan assumption, where someone assumes responsibility for the loan. However, most conventional loans are not assumable, meaning they include a due-on-sale clause that requires full repayment if ownership changes. Your loan is a conventional loan, so assumption would not be permitted.
Caller: So my realistic options are to either refinance and lose the great rate, or just add her to the title?
Agent: That's a fair summary. Many couples in this situation choose to add the spouse to the title via a new deed, which gives them legal co-ownership of the property, but leave the mortgage as-is under just one name. The non-borrower spouse would have full ownership rights to the property even if they're not on the loan.
