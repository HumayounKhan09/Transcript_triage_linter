Agent: Of course. A fraudulent conveyance — sometimes called a fraudulent transfer — is a transfer of property or assets made with the intent to hinder, delay, or defraud creditors. It's governed by the Uniform Fraudulent Transfer Act or the Uniform Voidable Transactions Act depending on the state, as well as by federal bankruptcy law in some contexts. The core idea is that people can't give away assets simply to put them out of the reach of people they owe money to.
Caller: So if someone owed money and then gave away their house to avoid paying?

If your grandmother owed a creditor — let's say she had a judgment against her for an unpaid debt — and then transferred the house to you or someone else to avoid that creditor seizing it, a court could undo that transfer and restore the property to your grandmother's estate so the creditor can be paid. That undoing is called voiding or setting aside the conveyance.
Caller: What if the transfer was innocent — what if she just wanted to give me the house as a gift?
Agent: Intent is one of the factors courts look at, but there's also what's called a constructive fraudulent conveyance, which doesn't require proof of bad intent. If the person making the transfer was insolvent at the time — meaning their debts exceeded their assets — and they transferred property without receiving reasonably equivalent value in exchange, that can be deemed fraudulent even without malicious intent. A gift, by definition, doesn't involve value in exchange.
