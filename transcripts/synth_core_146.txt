Caller: Hi Amanda. My name is Jerome Hutchins. I've had a terrible experience with my mortgage servicer and someone suggested I file a complaint with the CFPB. I don't really understand what that is or what it does. Can you walk me through it?
Agent: Hi Jerome, absolutely. The CFPB — Consumer Financial Protection Bureau — is the federal agency that oversees consumer financial products and services, including mortgages. Filing a complaint with them is a legitimate and often effective tool for resolving issues with servicers. What happened with your servicer?
Caller: Where do I start. They lost two of my payments and reported me as 30 days late to the credit bureaus even though I have proof the payments were made. Then when I tried to get it corrected, they were completely unresponsive for months. My credit score dropped 80 points and I lost a car loan opportunity because of it.
Agent: Jerome, what you're describing — being improperly reported as delinquent to the credit bureaus when you have proof of payment — is a serious issue. That can implicate multiple federal laws, including the Fair Credit Reporting Act and RESPA. Filing with the CFPB is absolutely the right move here. Let me walk you through the process.
Caller: Please. I've never done anything like this before.
Agent: The CFPB complaint process is at consumerfinance.gov/complaint. You can submit online, which is the fastest method. You can also call 855-411-2372 if you prefer to submit by phone. The submission is free and you don't need an attorney to file. You'll create an account, describe the issue in your own words, and the CFPB will forward the complaint to the company named.
Caller: What happens after they receive it?
Agent: After you submit, the CFPB sends the complaint to the company — your servicer — and asks them to respond. Companies are generally expected to respond within 15 days. The CFPB publishes these responses in a consumer complaint database, which is publicly searchable. Once the company responds, you'll receive a notification and can review their response and mark whether the issue was resolved to your satisfaction.
Caller: Does the CFPB actually do anything with complaints, or is it just paperwork?
Agent: Both. On an individual level, the complaint process often motivates companies to take the issue more seriously than they did when dealing with you directly. A company with CFPB oversight has regulatory incentive to respond promptly and resolve legitimate complaints. Many consumers report that issues they'd been fighting for months suddenly get resolved within weeks of a CFPB complaint.
Caller: That would be amazing. Mine has been going on for six months.
Agent: Six months is far too long for a payment posting error, and that kind of prolonged lack of resolution is exactly what the complaint system is designed to address. On the systemic level, the CFPB tracks complaint patterns. If many consumers report the same problem with the same servicer, that can trigger a supervisory examination or enforcement action. Large servicers have faced significant fines and penalties based on patterns of consumer complaints.
Caller: Good. They deserve it.
Agent: One thing that will make your complaint stronger is documentation. When you submit, include as much detail as possible: the dates the payments were made, the amounts, how you made the payments — check, electronic transfer, etc. — copies of your bank statements showing the debits, and copies of any correspondence with the servicer. If you have certified mail receipts, include those too.
Caller: I have all of that. I've been obsessively keeping records since this started.
Agent: That is the best thing you could have done. Your documentation is your evidence. A few specifics to include in the complaint: the exact dates and amounts of the two payments the servicer claims weren't received, the date you first contacted them to dispute, a log of every subsequent contact attempt and what response you received, the date the derogatory credit reporting appeared on your credit report, and the approximate credit score drop.
Caller: I have all of those dates documented in a spreadsheet. Should I mention the car loan I lost?
Agent: Absolutely. Include any concrete financial harm you suffered as a result of the inaccurate reporting. The lost car loan is a direct consequence and strengthens the impact of your complaint. Mention the date you applied, that you were denied or given unfavorable terms, and cite the credit impact as the cause.
Caller: Should I also file a complaint with the credit bureaus directly?
Agent: Yes, that's an important parallel step. You should dispute the inaccurate late payment with all three credit bureaus — Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion. You can do this online through each bureau's website. When you dispute, identify the specific tradeline — the servicer's account on your credit report — and the specific months marked as late that are inaccurate. Include your proof of payment as documentation. The bureau must investigate within 30 days and notify you of the result.
Caller: If the credit bureau investigates and the servicer still says I was late, what happens?
Agent: The credit bureau will typically side with whatever the furnisher — your servicer — reports. That's why the CFPB complaint is so important. If the CFPB flags the issue and the servicer is under scrutiny, they're more likely to correct their records with the credit bureaus, which should then trigger a correction on your report.
Caller: And if they still won't correct it?
Agent: Then you have grounds for a lawsuit under the Fair Credit Reporting Act against both the credit bureau and the servicer if they failed to adequately investigate your dispute. FCRA violations can result in actual damages, statutory damages of up to $1,000 per violation, and attorney fees. You'd want to consult with a consumer protection attorney who handles FCRA and RESPA cases.
Caller: I keep hearing about these attorney fees provisions. How do I find a FCRA or RESPA attorney?
Agent: Several ways. The National Association of Consumer Advocates at consumeradvocates.org has a directory of consumer protection attorneys. You can search by specialty and state. State bar associations also have referral services. Many of these attorneys work on contingency — no upfront cost — specifically because the fee-shifting statutes make it viable for them.
Caller: One more question — can I file complaints in multiple places at once?
Agent: Yes, you can file with the CFPB, the credit bureaus, your state's banking regulator, and your state attorney general all simultaneously. There's no rule against parallel complaints and it often increases pressure on the servicer to resolve things quickly.

Agent: That's a very common feeling and unfortunately a lot of consumers don't know these remedies exist. You have strong documentation, clear financial harm, and legitimate legal grounds to pursue this. Is there anything else I can help you with today, Jerome?
