Agent: It is definitely not a bill. It's an informational document that we're required to provide every year under a federal law called the Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act, commonly known as RESPA. Every mortgage servicer that manages an escrow account must send this statement to borrowers annually.
Caller: Okay. So what does it actually tell me?
Agent: There are two parts. The first part is a history section that looks back at the past twelve months of your escrow account. It shows, month by month, how much was collected into your escrow, how much was paid out — typically for property taxes and homeowners insurance — and what the actual balance was in the account each month.
Caller: I see that on the front page. It has a table with columns.
Agent: Exactly. The left column shows projected collections and disbursements — what we expected — and the right column shows actual activity — what actually happened. If those numbers differ, this document explains why.
Caller: And the second part?
Agent: The second part looks forward to the next twelve months. It's a projection of what we expect to collect and disburse in the coming year. This is based on your current tax and insurance obligations. At the end, it shows your projected escrow balance going forward and whether your current monthly escrow contribution is sufficient.
Caller: Mine shows something about a payment change. My payment is going up seventeen dollars.
Agent: That's the monthly adjustment. After analyzing the past year's actual activity and projecting next year's costs, we recalculate what you need to contribute monthly to have enough to cover taxes and insurance when they're due, plus maintain a required cushion. If taxes or insurance went up, your monthly escrow contribution goes up accordingly.
Caller: My insurance did renew at a higher premium. I remember thinking it went up at renewal.
Agent: That's almost certainly the cause. When insurance premiums increase, we need to collect more each month to make sure the premium can be paid when it comes due. Seventeen dollars a month is consistent with a modest insurance increase.
Caller: Does this change take effect automatically?
Agent: Yes, it's automatic. No action needed from you. The adjusted payment amount will apply starting with the payment date shown in the statement. The statement should include an effective date for the new payment amount.
