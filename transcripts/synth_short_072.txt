Agent: Correct. Forbearance provides temporary relief but the obligation remains. How the missed payments are repaid depends on the servicer and your loan type. It could be a lump sum at the end, added to the back of the loan, or spread out over future payments.
Caller: Okay. And what's a deferral then?
Agent: A deferral is a more specific resolution — it's often what happens after a forbearance period ends. With a deferral, the payments you skipped during forbearance are taken and moved to the end of your loan as a non-interest-bearing balance. So rather than paying a lump sum or having your monthly payment increase, those missed payments just get tacked on to the end and your regular monthly payment resumes as normal.
Caller: Oh, so a deferral is like a specific way of handling what you owe after forbearance?
Agent: That's a very good way to put it. Forbearance is the relief period itself, and deferral is often the repayment method used afterward. Though deferral can also be offered standalone in some cases for a single missed or skipped payment.
Caller: Which one is better?
Agent: It depends on your situation. Forbearance gives you breathing room during a hardship. Deferral is generally a favorable repayment option because it doesn't require a lump sum and doesn't increase your monthly payment — you just extend the loan slightly. In contrast, some resolution options after forbearance do increase your monthly payment, which can be harder to manage.
Caller: Can they affect your credit?
Agent: Forbearance, when properly documented and agreed to, generally does not result in late payment reporting to credit bureaus — though this can depend on the servicer and the terms of the forbearance agreement.
