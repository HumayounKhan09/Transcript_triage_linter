Agent: Hi Carla, I'm sorry you're dealing with this. RESPA — the Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act — is indeed a federal law that governs escrow accounts and mortgage servicing, and it does provide private rights of action for borrowers. I'm not an attorney, but I can explain the law and how it applies to servicers, and then you can determine if you want to pursue a claim. Can you tell me what specifically happened with your escrow?
Caller: Sure. For three years running, my servicer has done the annual escrow analysis late and then hit me with large shortage amounts that I had no time to plan for. Last year the shortage was $1,847, which they demanded in one lump sum. The year before, it was $1,400. I've also had my homeowner's insurance lapse twice because they didn't pay the premium on time even though I had funds in my escrow account.
Agent: Those are exactly the kinds of issues RESPA addresses. Let me break down the relevant provisions. RESPA, specifically Section 10, regulates escrow accounts. Servicers are required to maintain escrow accounts within certain limits — they can only collect a cushion of up to two months of escrow payments above what's needed. They must also conduct an annual escrow analysis and notify you of any shortage. There are rules about how they can collect shortage amounts.
Caller: They told me I had to pay the $1,847 upfront. Is that legal?
Agent: Under RESPA, if there's a shortage in your escrow account, the servicer has a choice. They can require you to repay the shortage in one lump sum, or they can spread the recovery over 12 months by increasing your monthly payment. They cannot be required to offer the 12-month option for shortages below $50, but above that amount the regulations do address how collection is handled. If your servicer demanded the full amount in one lump sum without offering a 12-month repayment option for a shortage of that size, that may be a violation depending on the specific regulation and circumstances.
Caller: They never offered me the monthly option. I had to scramble to come up with that money.
Agent: That's worth examining with an attorney. Regarding the insurance lapse issue — that's potentially more serious. Section 6 of RESPA requires servicers to respond promptly to qualified written requests, and they have obligations regarding timely payment of escrow items. If they had the funds in your escrow and failed to pay the insurance premium on time, causing a lapse in coverage, that is a potential RESPA violation. And beyond RESPA, if you suffered any harm due to the lapse — say a claim was denied because of the lapse — you might have additional state law tort claims.
Caller: During the second lapse, I had a small roof damage claim and the insurance company initially denied it because of the lapse. It eventually got resolved but I had to spend weeks fighting it.
Agent: That's exactly the type of consequential harm that could support a damages claim. Let me explain RESPA Section 6 more specifically. Section 6 requires that servicers acknowledge a qualified written request within 5 business days and respond substantively within 30 business days. Did you ever send written complaints about these issues to your servicer?
Caller: I sent emails. Does that count?
Agent: Email can qualify as a qualified written request under RESPA if it includes your name, account number, and a description of the issue and is sent to the proper address. However, RESPA specifies that the QWR should be sent to the servicer's designated address for receiving such requests — some servicers have a specific QWR processing address that's different from general correspondence. If your emails went to a general customer service address rather than the designated QWR address, the servicer might argue the formal QWR response requirements didn't trigger.
Caller: I have no idea about a designated address. I just emailed their customer service.
Agent: This is actually a common trap. The designated QWR address is supposed to be disclosed on every periodic statement. Check your billing statements — there should be a notice or disclosure section with an address designated for qualified written requests. If they failed to disclose it, that itself could be a violation.
Caller: What damages could I recover if I sued?
Agent: Under RESPA Section 6, if a servicer fails to properly respond to a QWR, you can recover actual damages — meaning the harm you actually suffered — plus up to $2,000 in statutory damages per violation in cases of a pattern or practice of noncompliance. You can also recover attorney fees and costs if you prevail. In class actions, the statutory damages can be much higher — up to $1 million or 1 percent of the servicer's net worth.
Caller: If I'm not the only one this happened to, would a class action be something to look into?
Agent: If this servicer has been engaging in systematic escrow mishandling or repeatedly failing to pay insurance premiums on time across many accounts, a class action is certainly possible. This is something a consumer protection attorney would need to investigate. They would look at patterns, complaint records, regulatory actions against the servicer, and other borrowers' experiences.
Caller: Where do I start? Do I need a lawyer first?
Agent: I'd recommend a two-track approach. First, file a formal complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau at consumerfinance.gov. The CFPB supervises mortgage servicers and their complaint process can sometimes result in the servicer correcting errors and compensating you. The CFPB also shares complaint data with state regulators. Second, consult with a consumer protection attorney who handles RESPA claims. Many work on contingency because RESPA allows fee-shifting. You might also contact your state's banking regulator with a complaint.
Caller: Before I do any of that, should I send a formal qualified written request to the servicer?
Agent: Yes, and that should probably be your first step. Send a certified letter — return receipt requested — to the servicer's designated QWR address. In the letter, cite your loan number, describe the specific issues with dates and dollar amounts, and request a full accounting of your escrow account for the past three years including all disbursements and shortage calculations. Keep a copy of everything. Their response — or failure to respond — becomes part of your evidentiary record.
