Agent: That's the most important thing right now — locate your owner's title insurance policy. It should be in a document that says "Commitment for Title Insurance" or "Title Insurance Policy" from a company like First American, Fidelity National, or Old Republic. The policy number and the title insurance company name would be on the first page.
Caller: Why is the title insurance relevant?
Agent: Because boundary disputes and encroachments — which is what your neighbor is claiming with the fence and driveway — are typically covered under owner's title insurance. If your neighbor prevails in establishing that part of your fence or driveway encroaches on his property, you may have a claim against your title insurer.
Caller: So my title insurance would pay if I have to move the fence?
Agent: Potentially. Owner's title insurance typically covers survey defects, encroachments, and boundary issues that existed at the time of closing. If the boundary was not accurately reflected in the title report and you've suffered a loss as a result — such as having to relocate improvements — you could file a claim with the title company.
Caller: What about from the mortgage side — does this affect my loan?
Agent: From our perspective as the mortgage servicer, a boundary dispute itself doesn't immediately affect your loan. Your obligation to pay the mortgage continues regardless of the dispute. However, there are a few scenarios where it could become our concern.
Caller: Like what?
Agent: If the dispute escalates to a legal judgment that results in a court-ordered property line adjustment — meaning a court determines that a portion of what you thought was your land is legally your neighbor's — that could technically reduce the size of your collateral. In extreme cases, if a court ordered the removal of improvements that are on disputed land, that could affect the property's value.
Caller: My home is well within my surveyor's interpretation of the boundary. The dispute is only about four feet of fence and about six feet of driveway near the property line. My house itself is not in dispute.
Agent: In that case, the direct impact on your collateral is minimal. A four-foot strip along a property line is unlikely to materially affect the appraised value of the house itself. The dispute is more of a legal and neighbor relations matter than a mortgage matter.

Caller: What's a lis pendens and how does it affect things?
Agent: A lis pendens is a notice recorded in the county property records that alerts third parties that there's active litigation involving the property. It doesn't encumber the property like a lien does, but it does cloud the title and would complicate any sale or refinance until the lawsuit is resolved.
Caller: So if my neighbor sues me and files a lis pendens, I'd be stuck?
Agent: Not permanently, but until the lawsuit is resolved, yes. A sale or refinance would be challenging because title companies don't like insuring properties with active litigation. It would need to be resolved — either settled or adjudicated — before title would be considered clear.

Agent: That's the best outcome. A negotiated boundary agreement — sometimes called a boundary line agreement or a written stipulation recorded with the county — can resolve the dispute and put both parties on the same page without litigation.
Caller: Would a recorded boundary agreement satisfy your requirements if we ever needed to refinance?
Agent: Generally yes. A recorded boundary agreement that establishes the agreed-upon property line would be viewed favorably by a title company and a new lender. It resolves the ambiguity and clears the title.
