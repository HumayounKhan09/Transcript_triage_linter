Caller: It was an adjustable-rate mortgage at 6.875 percent. I thought I was getting a good rate but looking back, people in my neighborhood with similar credit got lower rates. The loan was through a broker — not directly with a bank.
Agent: That's consistent with how yield spread premiums worked. So let me explain the mechanics. A yield spread premium, or YSP, was a payment made by the wholesale lender to the mortgage broker in exchange for the broker placing you in a loan with a higher interest rate than you actually qualified for. In other words: if you qualified for a loan at 6 percent but the broker put you in a 6.875 percent loan, the lender would pay the broker a premium — often 1 to 2 percent of the loan amount — for the higher-rate loan. The lender profits more because you're paying more interest over time.
Caller: So the broker got paid extra for giving me a worse deal?
Agent: That's precisely how it worked. And the problem was that this payment was often not clearly disclosed to borrowers. They might see a line item in their closing documents but without a clear explanation of what it meant or how it affected their interest rate. The broker had an incentive to give you the highest rate you'd accept because they got paid more for it.
Caller: That makes me furious. How much money are we talking about?
Agent: The YSP was typically expressed as points — 1 percent of the loan amount per point. If your loan was, say, $250,000 and the YSP was 1.5 percent, the broker received $3,750 directly from the lender just for putting you in the higher-rate loan. Meanwhile, over the life of the loan, you'd pay significantly more in interest.
Caller: My original loan was $318,000. So if the YSP was 1.5 percent, the broker got nearly $5,000?
Agent: Yes, potentially in that ballpark. And you'd be paying a higher rate over the life of the loan. Even a 0.5 percent higher rate on $318,000 over 30 years can mean $30,000 to $50,000 more in total interest, depending on how long you kept the loan.
Caller: Is there anything I can do about it now? Is there any recourse?
Agent: This is where the situation gets complicated by time. Yield spread premiums themselves were legal prior to Dodd-Frank, though disclosure requirements existed. Under RESPA, yield spread premiums had to be disclosed on the HUD-1 settlement statement. If the YSP was not disclosed at all, that was a RESPA violation. If it was inadequately disclosed, there may also have been a TILA issue. However, the statute of limitations for RESPA claims is generally 1 to 3 years from the origination date. A 2006 loan is well outside that window in most cases.

Caller: Did they ever ban yield spread premiums?
Agent: Yes. The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act, signed in 2010, effectively banned compensation structures that incentivized brokers to give borrowers higher rates. Under the new rules, broker compensation must be either paid by the borrower or by the lender — not both — and cannot vary based on the loan's interest rate. So brokers today can't be paid more for giving you a higher rate. The regulation took effect for most loan types in 2011.
Caller: Better late than never, I suppose. So people getting mortgages today don't have this problem?
Agent: Not in the same form, no. The regulation significantly changed how mortgage broker compensation works. Brokers today must disclose their compensation clearly on the Loan Estimate and Closing Disclosure, and the total compensation they receive cannot be tied to the rate. It's not a perfect system but it's a major improvement over the pre-2008 era.
Caller: What should I look for in my old documents to confirm what happened to me?

If there was a YSP, it typically appeared as a credit — a negative number — in that section, with a notation like "YSP" or "lender-paid compensation" or sometimes just a credit from the lender. It should also have appeared on your Good Faith Estimate that was provided at application. If you see those entries, it confirms a YSP was paid.
