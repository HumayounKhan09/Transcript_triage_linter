The counseling requirement is something a lot of reverse mortgage applicants have questions about, and it's actually one of the most consumer-protective features of the HECM program. Caller: Yes, I've got time. I'm retired. I have plenty of time.
Agent: Wonderful. So let me start with why the counseling is required. The Home Equity Conversion Mortgage, which is the government-insured reverse mortgage, is regulated by HUD — the Department of Housing and Urban Development. Congress mandated that all HECM applicants receive independent counseling before they can apply. The reason is that reverse mortgages are complex financial products with long-term implications. The counseling ensures you're making an informed decision without pressure from any lender.
Caller: But the lender I spoke to was very helpful and I felt like I understood everything they told me.
Agent: I'm glad the lender was helpful. The key word is independent. The counselor has no financial stake in whether you proceed with the reverse mortgage. They're not trying to sell you anything. Their only job is to make sure you understand the product fully, the alternatives available to you, and any risks involved. They might actually point out something that leads you to reconsider or choose a different path — and that's by design.
Caller: What if I already know what I want?
Agent: Then the counseling will probably confirm your decision and give you the certificate you need to proceed. Most people who go in having done their research come out feeling more confident. But occasionally someone learns something in counseling they didn't know, and it changes their thinking. The goal is to make sure no one signs into a reverse mortgage they didn't fully understand.
Caller: Alright, that makes sense. What exactly happens during the counseling session?
Agent: The session is conducted by a HUD-approved housing counselor — an independent individual or agency certified specifically for HECM counseling. It's typically done over the phone, though some counselors do in-person sessions. It usually takes between 60 and 90 minutes. The counselor will cover the basics of how reverse mortgages work, including how interest accrues, when the loan becomes due, and the payment options available to you.
Caller: I know all of that already. Can I just tell them that?
Agent: The counselor will still go through the material with you — it's a required protocol. But you can indicate your level of familiarity and the session may move more efficiently as a result. They're not trying to waste your time; they're following a standardized curriculum developed by HUD.
Caller: What else do they cover?
Agent: Several important topics. They'll discuss your financial situation and whether a reverse mortgage is the most appropriate solution given your circumstances. They'll go over alternatives — things like property tax deferral programs, downsizing, grants for seniors, or other assistance programs that might address your needs without using your home equity.
Caller: I've looked into all of those. None of them work for my situation.
Agent: Then you'll be able to tell the counselor that and explain why. They'll make a note of it. They'll also cover the ongoing obligations of a reverse mortgage borrower — you're required to maintain the home, keep up with property taxes and homeowner's insurance, and use the home as your primary residence. Failure to meet those obligations can trigger the loan becoming due.
Caller: Yes, the lender told me about those.
Agent: Good. The counselor will also explain what happens to the loan when you pass away or move out — how the estate handles it, the 30-day notification requirement, the option for heirs to purchase the home at 95 percent of appraised value if they want to keep it. Does any of that sound new to you?
Caller: The 95 percent thing — can you explain that?
Agent: Sure. When a reverse mortgage becomes due — whether because the borrower passed away or moved out — the lender generally gives the estate a timeframe to resolve the loan. If an heir wants to keep the home, they have the option to pay off the loan balance or 95 percent of the appraised value, whichever is less. So if the loan balance has grown to $350,000 but the home is only worth $290,000, the heir would only need to pay 95 percent of $290,000 — which is $275,500 — to take possession of the home.
Caller: That's actually useful. I have a son who might want to keep the house someday.
Agent: That's exactly the kind of information the counseling session reinforces. It's valuable for your family planning as well as your own.
Caller: Now about the cost — you said there's a fee. How much?
Agent: The counseling fee is typically between $125 and $175. It's set by the individual counseling agency. By federal law, the agency cannot refuse to counsel you based on inability to pay — they must offer a fee waiver if you truly cannot afford it.
Caller: I can afford $125. I just wanted to know if it was going to be some large expense.
Agent: No, it's quite modest given what's involved. And some lenders will reimburse the counseling fee at closing if you proceed with the loan. You'd want to ask your specific lender about that.
Caller: How do I find an approved counselor?
Agent: You can call the HUD counseling hotline at 800-569-4287. They'll connect you with approved counselors in your area. You can also go to HUD's website and use the counselor search tool. Make sure the counselor is specifically certified for HECM counseling — not just general housing counseling.
Caller: Can my daughter come with me? She's been helping me with all of this.

I'd recommend calling HUD's hotline first thing tomorrow morning to get on someone's calendar. Once you complete counseling and receive your certificate, it's valid for 180 days — so you have time to shop lenders and finalize your application.
