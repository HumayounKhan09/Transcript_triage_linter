Agent: Forbearance is a temporary pause or reduction in your mortgage payments. The key word is temporary. During a forbearance period, you're allowed to pay less — sometimes nothing — for a defined period, typically three to six months, sometimes up to twelve months in some programs. The important thing to understand is that forbearance is not forgiveness. Those payments you skip or reduce are still owed. They don't disappear.
Caller: So I'd owe it all at the end?
Agent: That's where it gets nuanced. After forbearance ends, there are several ways the deferred amount can be handled. One option is a lump-sum repayment — you pay back everything you missed at once, which is rarely feasible for someone coming out of financial hardship. A more common option is a repayment plan, where the deferred amount is spread out over a period of months added on top of your regular payment. Another option — and this is very common now — is deferral, where the missed payments are moved to the end of your loan as a non-interest-bearing balance, due when you sell or pay off the home. The specific option depends on your loan type and investor guidelines.
Caller: That last one sounds much better than a lump sum.
Agent: For most people it is. Now let me explain modification. A loan modification is a permanent change to the terms of your existing loan. Unlike forbearance, which is a temporary accommodation, modification actually restructures the loan. That might mean reducing the interest rate, extending the loan term from 20 years to 30 years for example, or in some cases deferring a portion of the principal. The goal is to permanently lower your monthly payment to something affordable based on your income.
