Agent: Absolutely, Mr. Huang, and it's actually a really common question because that language in the denial letter can feel very abstract. Let me walk you through what the NPV test is and what it means for your situation. NPV stands for net present value. In the context of loan modifications, it's a standardized financial model that servicers are required to use to evaluate whether modifying a loan makes financial sense from the investor's perspective.
Caller: So it's about whether the bank makes money?
Agent: Essentially, yes — but it's more nuanced than that. The model compares two scenarios. Scenario one is the expected value to the investor if the loan is modified. Scenario two is the expected value to the investor if the loan goes through foreclosure. The model discounts those future cash flows back to today's dollars — that's the present value part — and compares them. If modification produces a higher net present value than foreclosure, the test passes. If foreclosure produces a higher expected return, the test fails.
Caller: So in my case, the model said they'd do better in foreclosure than if they modified my loan?
Agent: That's what the calculation showed, yes. But it's important to understand that the NPV model uses specific inputs, and if any of those inputs were incorrect, it can affect the outcome. Under the rules that govern servicers — for conventional loans under GSE guidelines — you have the right to know what inputs were used in the NPV analysis and to challenge them if you believe they're inaccurate.
Caller: What kind of inputs go into it?
Agent: There are several key variables. The current market value of your property is a big one — if the estimate used was lower than the actual market value, that could depress the modification scenario's NPV. Your current income and proposed modified monthly payment are also inputs, as is your current interest rate, the proposed modified rate, the remaining loan term, the local foreclosure cost and timeline estimates, and probability-of-default assumptions. There's also a reinstatement probability — the likelihood you'd pay off the loan without modification.
Caller: How do they estimate my home's value? Did they get an appraisal?
Agent: In many cases, servicers use automated valuation models — AVMs — rather than a full appraisal. An AVM is an algorithm that estimates value based on comparable sales, and it can sometimes be off. If you believe your home is worth more than what was used in the NPV model, that's actually one of the strongest grounds for appeal.
