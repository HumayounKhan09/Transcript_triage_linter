Agent: You have several options for how you receive the funds, and that's one of the things that makes the HECM program flexible. Option one is a lump sum payment, but this is only available with a fixed interest rate, and the fixed rate option limits you to taking one draw at the time of closing. Option two is a line of credit, which most financial planners actually consider the most powerful feature of the HECM. With a line of credit, you only draw what you need when you need it, and here's the unique part: the unused portion of your line of credit grows over time based on the interest rate. So if you set up a $250,000 line and only draw $50,000 initially, that remaining $200,000 keeps growing in availability even as your home's value might fluctuate.
Caller: Wait, the line of credit grows? I've never heard of that.
Agent: It's one of the least understood but most valuable features. It's called the line of credit growth rate, and it equals the interest rate plus the 0.5 percent annual MIP rate. So in practical terms, if your interest rate is 6 percent, your available line grows at roughly 6.5 percent annually. That means the longer you wait to draw from it, the more you have access to. For someone who takes this out at 73 and doesn't need to draw heavily for several years, the available credit can grow substantially.

Agent: The third option is monthly payments, and there are two types. Tenure payments give you a fixed monthly payment for as long as you live in the home, regardless of how long that is. Term payments give you a fixed monthly payment for a specified number of months that you choose. You can also combine options, for example taking a partial lump sum at closing to pay off any existing debts, and then setting up a line of credit or monthly payments for the remaining balance.
Caller: I don't have any existing debts, so that's not an issue. My next question is about interest. How does the interest work since I'm not making payments?
Agent: Great question. On a HECM, the interest accrues monthly and is added to your loan balance. So the balance grows over time rather than shrinking. The interest rate can be either fixed, which only applies to the lump sum option, or adjustable, which applies to all the other options. The adjustable rate is tied to an index, typically the Constant Maturity Treasury rate or SOFR, plus a margin set by the lender. These rates are capped, though. There's typically a 2 percent annual cap, meaning the rate can't jump more than 2 percent in any single year, and there's a lifetime cap of 5 percent above your initial rate.
Caller: Okay. And I'll never owe more than the house is worth, right? I've heard that.
Agent: That's correct, and it's actually one of the most important protections of the HECM program. It's called the non-recourse feature. When the loan comes due, whether because you move out, sell the house, or pass away, the lender can only collect the value of the home. If the loan balance exceeds what the home sells for, FHA mortgage insurance covers the difference. You, your heirs, and your estate will never be personally liable for any shortfall.
Caller: My daughter's main worry is that I'll lose the house. What are the things that could cause me to lose the house?
Agent: This is really important to understand. There are specific conditions called maturity events that cause the loan to become due and payable. First, if you move out of the home as your primary residence for more than twelve consecutive months. This includes moving into assisted living or a nursing facility. Second, if you pass away. Third, if you sell the home. Fourth, if you fail to maintain the home in good condition. Fifth, if you fail to pay property taxes. And sixth, if you fail to maintain required homeowner's insurance. The tax and insurance requirements are the most common reason people get into trouble with reverse mortgages. If you don't pay your property taxes, the lender can call the loan due.

The LESA is essentially a portion of your loan proceeds set aside in an escrow account specifically to pay taxes and insurance. Caller: I plan to stay until I can't anymore. Okay, what about my heirs? What happens to the house when I die?
Agent: When you pass away, your heirs have several options. They have typically sixty days, extendable to twelve months with communication and documentation, to decide what to do. They can sell the home and use the proceeds to pay off the loan, keeping any remaining equity. They can refinance the home with a traditional mortgage to pay off the HECM balance. Or they can purchase the home from the estate for the lesser of the outstanding loan balance or ninety-five percent of the appraised value. If the balance is less than the home value, there will be equity left for your heirs. If the balance exceeds the value, FHA insurance covers the difference and your heirs owe nothing.
