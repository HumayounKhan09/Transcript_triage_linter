Agent: Hi Franklin, this is definitely something worth understanding before you close on your sale. A recapture tax is a federal tax provision that applies to borrowers who used mortgage revenue bond loans — sometimes called state bond loans or MRB loans — when they purchased their home. It's administered by the IRS, not by the lender, so I want to make sure I explain clearly where my role ends and where your tax professional's guidance begins.
Caller: Of course. But can you explain the basic mechanics?
Agent: Absolutely. Mortgage revenue bonds are a financing tool that states use to offer below-market interest rates to first-time homebuyers with moderate incomes. The federal government subsidizes these programs. In exchange for that subsidy, the IRS includes a recapture provision in the tax code — Section 143 of the Internal Revenue Code. The idea is that if you profit significantly from selling the home within nine years, you repay a portion of the subsidy you received.
Caller: Nine years? I've only been in the house for six years. So I'm in the window.
Agent: Yes, the recapture window is nine years from the date of origination. If you sell within that period and meet certain other conditions, the recapture tax could apply. However — and this is important — just being in the nine-year window doesn't automatically mean you owe the tax. There are three conditions that must all be met simultaneously.
Caller: What are the three conditions?
Agent: First, you must sell the property within nine years of the closing date of the MRB loan. Second, you must realize a gain on the sale — meaning you sell for more than your adjusted basis in the property. Third, your income in the year of sale must exceed a threshold called the federally subsidized income limit, which is based on your family size and the area. If your income in the year of sale is at or below that limit, no recapture applies regardless of gain.
Caller: What's the income limit?
Agent: The income limits are set at origination and indexed to a federal threshold, typically 150 to 200 percent of area median income for your jurisdiction and family size. I don't have the specific limit from your loan file — that should be in your original loan documents or your state housing finance agency can provide it. Can I ask — has your income changed significantly since you purchased the home six years ago?
Caller: My household income was around $65,000 when I bought. Now it's closer to $110,000.
Agent: That increase is relevant. Higher income makes it more likely you'd exceed the limit, which means the first condition is potentially met. But you'd need the exact income limit figure from your state program to know for sure.
