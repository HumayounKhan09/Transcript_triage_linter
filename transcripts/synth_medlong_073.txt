Agent: The HECM reverse mortgage is designed specifically for homeowners 62 and older, so you qualify at 68. The key distinguishing feature is that there are no required monthly payments as long as you live in the home. The loan accrues interest over time and is repaid when you sell, move, or pass away. On a home worth $520,000 at your age, you might have access to a principal limit in the range of $260,000 to $290,000, depending on current interest rates at the time of application.
Caller: So I could take out $260,000 with no monthly payment?
Agent: That's the mechanics, yes. You can take it as a lump sum, as a line of credit, as monthly payments, or combinations. For retirement income planning, the line of credit is often most powerful because the unused portion grows over time. So if you took a $260,000 line and only drew $20,000 per year, the remaining balance keeps growing in availability, potentially significantly, over a decade or more.
Caller: That growth feature is interesting. Is there a catch?
Agent: The costs and the long-term balance growth are the main considerations. The upfront costs are significant, around 2 to 3 percent of the loan amount in fees and mortgage insurance. The interest that accrues is added to the balance, so the balance grows over time. If you live in the home for twenty-five years and draw heavily, the balance could consume most or all of the equity. However, the non-recourse feature guarantees you'll never owe more than the home's value at sale.

In many scenarios, especially if home values appreciate over time, there's still equity remaining even after a reverse mortgage is repaid.
Caller: You mentioned something called a home equity investment. What's that?
Agent: This is a newer category of product offered by several fintech companies. A home equity investment, or HEI, is not a loan in the traditional sense. The investor gives you a lump sum of cash today in exchange for a percentage of your home's future appreciation or value at the time of sale. You owe no monthly payments and no interest. But when you eventually sell the home, the investor takes their agreed-upon percentage of the value, not just the appreciation in some structures.
Caller: So they get a share of my house?

If you sell in ten years at $700,000, the investor receives $140,000. If the home drops in value, they share in the loss. There's no interest accruing, which sounds appealing, but the cost can be very high if your home appreciates significantly.
