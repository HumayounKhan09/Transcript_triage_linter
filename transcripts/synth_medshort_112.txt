Caller: Please, because I've never heard that term before.
Agent: QM, or Qualified Mortgage, is a category of mortgage loans defined by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau under rules that came out of the Dodd-Frank Act following the 2008 financial crisis. The rules were designed to ensure lenders verify that borrowers have the ability to repay their loans.
Caller: So it's about whether they checked my income?
Agent: Partly. A Qualified Mortgage has several criteria. The lender must verify your income, assets, and debts. The loan can't have certain risky features like negative amortization, interest-only periods beyond a certain point, or balloon payments in most cases. And the term can't exceed thirty years. There's also a points and fees cap.
Caller: What's the points and fees cap?
Agent: For most loans, the total points and fees at origination can't exceed three percent of the loan amount to be a QM. There are some exceptions for smaller loan amounts.
Caller: And why does my attorney care about this?
Agent: In a divorce, one party may be assuming the loan or you may be refinancing. The QM status can matter because it gives you certain consumer protections. If a lender makes a QM loan that meets all the requirements, there's a presumption — sometimes conclusive, sometimes rebuttable — that the lender complied with the ability-to-repay requirements. This protects borrowers in certain legal proceedings.
Caller: So if I wanted to sue the lender for not checking my ability to repay, QM status matters?
Agent: Exactly. If your loan is a QM, the lender has a legal safe harbor or at least a presumption of compliance. If it's not a QM, the lender's compliance is more easily challenged. Your attorney may be exploring all angles related to your mortgage situation in the divorce.
