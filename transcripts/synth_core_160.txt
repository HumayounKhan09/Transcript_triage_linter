Agent: A residential appraisal is conducted by a licensed, independent appraiser who is assigned by our appraisal management company. We don't directly select the appraiser — they're assigned independently to avoid any conflict of interest. The appraiser visits your home, inspects the condition and features, and then develops an opinion of value using multiple methods.
Caller: What methods do they use?
Agent: The primary method for a single-family home is the sales comparison approach. The appraiser finds recently sold properties in your area that are similar to your home — these are called comparable sales, or comps. They look at homes that sold within the past six to twelve months, ideally within a mile of your property, and compare them to yours.
Caller: What makes a home comparable?
Agent: Appraisers look for homes similar in size, age, construction, style, and condition. They compare square footage, number of bedrooms and bathrooms, garage, lot size, basement, and upgrades. When a comp has features your home doesn't — like a pool — the appraiser makes a downward adjustment to that comp's value. When your home has something the comp lacks, they make an upward adjustment.
Caller: So they're adjusting the sale prices of other homes to estimate mine?
Agent: Exactly. After adjusting all the comps, the appraiser weighs them and arrives at an opinion of value for your home. A typical appraisal uses three to six comparables, and the appraiser reconciles them to give a final number.
Caller: What if there aren't many recent sales in my neighborhood?
Agent: In lower-turnover areas, the appraiser may need to expand the search area — going to a two or three mile radius or extending the time range to 12 months or even 18 months in some cases. They'll note the limited comparability and may adjust their confidence accordingly. In rural areas this gets more complex, but appraisers are trained to work within their guidelines.
Caller: What can I do to help get the best possible value?
Agent: Great question. There are things within your control. Make sure the home is clean, well-maintained, and accessible on the inspection date. Any obvious deferred maintenance — peeling paint, broken fixtures, damaged flooring — can negatively impact the condition rating, which affects value. Fix small things before the appraiser arrives if you can.
Caller: What about improvements I've made? Like we put in a new kitchen last year.
Agent: Improvements are very relevant, but you need to document them for the appraiser. Prepare a list of any significant upgrades in the last five years — new HVAC, roof, kitchen, bathrooms, windows, flooring — and note the approximate cost and year completed. Give this list to the appraiser at the start of the inspection. They can't give credit for something they don't know about.
Caller: That's helpful. What about the size of the home? We converted our garage to a finished room a few years ago. Does that count?
Agent: Finished square footage is one of the biggest value drivers. However, the converted space may or may not be counted in the gross living area depending on how it was finished and whether it has proper permits. Appraisers follow specific guidelines — space must be heated, finished, and connected to the main living area to count as GLA. If the garage conversion was done properly, it should count and boost your value.
Caller: I'm not sure if it was permitted. Does that matter?
Agent: It can. Unpermitted additions may be discounted or excluded by the appraiser depending on local practices. Some appraisers will count the space but note it was unpermitted; others won't count it at all. It's worth checking your county records before the appraisal date. If you can pull a permit retroactively, that helps.
Caller: What if the appraisal comes in low? What happens to my refinance?
Agent: If the appraised value is lower than needed, it affects your loan-to-value ratio. For example, if you need an 80 percent LTV for the best rates, and the appraisal comes in lower than you expected, you might fall into a higher LTV bracket, which could mean a higher rate or PMI. In some cases, if the value is too low, the refinance may not pencil out.
Caller: What can I do if I think the appraisal is wrong?
Agent: You have the right to receive a copy of the appraisal — that's required by law. Review it carefully. Look at the comps used and check whether they're truly comparable to your home. If you believe the appraiser made errors — used an incorrect square footage, missed a major upgrade, or used comps that aren't representative — you can submit a formal reconsideration of value, or ROV.
Caller: What does that involve?
Agent: You'd provide specific information: corrected data points, comps the appraiser might have overlooked that sold at higher prices, or documentation that the appraiser got a fact wrong. We submit the ROV to the appraiser through the appraisal management company. The appraiser reviews and may or may not revise their opinion. It's not guaranteed, but it's a legitimate process.
Caller: Can I just get a second appraisal?
Agent: You can order your own appraisal for your own information, but lenders typically require they use the appraisal they ordered through their management company. Paying for a second appraisal independently generally doesn't replace the lender's report. The ROV process is the formal channel.
