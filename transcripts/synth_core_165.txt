Caller: Banned? Like by law?
Agent: Effectively, yes. The Dodd-Frank Act of 2010 established what's called the Ability to Repay rule, which requires lenders to make a reasonable and good faith determination that a borrower can repay the loan before extending credit. Allowing a borrower to simply state an income without verification violates that rule. Lenders who originate qualified mortgages — which carry important legal protections — must verify income through documented means.
Caller: So if someone is offering a stated income loan today, that's a red flag?
Agent: It could be. There are some non-QM products — non-qualified mortgages — that don't follow every QM guideline, and some of them have reduced income documentation requirements. But even those require some form of income verification, just through alternative means rather than tax returns. If you hear someone offering a true no-documentation, state-your-income loan with no verification at all, I'd encourage extreme caution. That's either a misrepresentation of the product or potentially a predatory situation.
Caller: That's concerning. So what happened in the 2000s exactly?
Agent: Without going too deep into history, stated income loans — sometimes called liar loans in retrospect — allowed borrowers to claim any income on their application without the lender verifying it. Combined with inflated home values and other risky practices, they contributed significantly to the 2008 housing market collapse. A lot of borrowers ended up in loans they couldn't actually afford once adjustable rates reset, and lenders had no way to know at origination whether the borrowers could really repay.

For businesses with higher overhead — like construction or retail — the factor might be 60 to 70 percent, so they'd count 30 to 40 percent of deposits. The rationale is that not every deposit is profit, so they account for business expenses the statements don't show.
